Young Tech Professionals
and Pottery

by Alyssa Frederick, Ashley Kim, and Taewon Yoon

Third Spaces

When Michela Ferrari moved to San Francisco, she didn't expect pottery to become such a big part of her life. As a 27-year-old product designer at Comun, a financial technology company, she spends most of her days in front of a screen. But in 2021, nearing the end of the COVID-19 pandemic, she decided to try something new. “I kind of wanted something to do other than working from home,” she said. Her boyfriend at the time—now husband—encouraged her to give pottery a try.

“You’d really enjoy it,” he told her, drawing from his own experience with pottery in art school. “He was like, you should try it out.”

For Ferrari, pottery became more than just a hobby; it became a third space. “Especially when I was living close to my studio, it was so easy to go there and spend time... it gave me a sense of ownership, like I belong here,” she said.

Third spaces are spaces outside of home and work where people gather and establish community. They are particularly relevant for young tech workers in San Francisco, many of whom work remotely and don’t have established communities.

Tommy Li, a 26-year-old product designer at Atlassian, a collaboration-focused software company, shares Ferrari’s sentiment. “Pottery feels like a third space for me,” he said. Like Ferrari, Li spends most of his day in front of a screen—be it at the office or at home—and pottery offers a break from the digital world.

Social Spaces

Meg Frazier, a program manager at Adobe in her late 20s, agreed with Li’s sentiment. “Pottery gets me out of the house,” she said. “After college, I wasn’t hanging out in communal spaces with friends as often as I used to, and after COVID, the motivation for me to go out was even less.”

Though Frazier works in-person twice a week, a majority of her work is done online, like Li’s. Hitting the bar with colleagues after work isn’t uncommon, but Frazier pointed out that pottery is a nice space to have as someone who doesn’t necessarily enjoy drinking all the time. She also likes having a space separate from her friend group and work life where she can meet people.

For some, pottery is a social activity, while for others, it’s a quiet retreat. Frazier has noticed a mix of both. “Some people come in with headphones and others want to make friends.” Michela, who has been taking classes for years, has built relationships with classmates and studio owners. She initially started going with friends, but over time, they stopped coming while she kept going. “But it was nice because you would make friends in your classes,” she said. “(Even though) I wouldn't consider them necessarily like my friends, it's people you know, they’ll ask you what you’re working on.” She also enjoys the variety of ages that appear in pottery studios. “There's a lot of older people that are retired … And it's nice to have an older person to talk to, and sometimes they'll bring in their dogs,” Ferrari mentioned.

Some people bring friends with them, whether to share the experience or collaborate. Li once invited a friend who wasn’t a studio member to help him finish abandoned pieces. “I had a bunch of stuff I hadn't glazed, and I just had her paint on a piece I made and had no plans to finish. It's cool to have a collaboration opportunity and to be able to use that space to interact with people I know,” Li said.

Cost of Pottery

While pottery offers a creative and social aspect, it can be costly. Li initially hesitated to sign up because of the price. “It’s not a cheap hobby,” he said. “But eventually I signed up, and once I started, I really enjoyed it. I’ve been doing pottery for about eight months now.”

He also acknowledges the exclusivity that comes with the expense. “The thing about it is that it’s an expensive hobby. People who can afford to have that kind of space—it feels kind of bad, you know?”

Ferrari also acknowledged that pottery is expensive, but has found workarounds to make it fit in her budget. “Typically for the three hour class, it’s $200. So I never tried that, because the six week class is $350 and I was like, I’m just going to fully commit and do the expensive one,” she said. “I just try to do it when I know I’m going to have dedicated time and be physically present.”

Pottery vs. Other Hobbies

For Ferrari, pottery stands out from her other hobbies, like rug-making and drawing, because of its unique challenges and rewards. “A lot of other hobbies are easy to pick up, at least for me, and once you get the hang of it, you might lose interest,” she said. “But pottery is a never ending challenge. You can always do more stuff and expand.”

Li pairs pottery with volleyball. Seeing the two hobbies as complementary. When the interviewer compared their childhood hobbies to having one active and one creative hobby, Li agreed. “It kind of sounds similar to having pottery and volleyball,” he said. “Yeah definitely.” For Li, having both creative and physical hobbies has helped him maintain a healthy work-life balance and even increased his productivity at work. “Having multiple things I care about outside of work has actually helped with my work ethic,” he explained. “Before I got really invested in these hobbies, I’d just keep working past 5 p.m. without much progress. Now, I schedule and set goals to finish on time so I can make it to the studio or volleyball. It’s made me more productive during the workday and get more out of my literal nine-to-five.”

Favorite Creations/Satisfaction

Corgi Cup

For Ferrari, one of her favorite creations is a corgi cup. “I made a little cup that has a corgi inside... when you drink, the corgi kind of touches your nose, which is adorable,” she said. She also takes pride in her collection of tableware. “Every time I eat from one of the plates I've made, I feel a sense of satisfaction,” she shared.

Large Bowl

One of Li’s favorite pieces is a large bowl by his front door that he uses to hold his Lactaid pills. “It was a good opportunity to make something at the studio that can be used in my home,” Li said. Meg also finds joy in her pottery collection. She’s made all of the dishware in her home.

Something Cool

For all three, the satisfaction of creating and using their handmade pieces is fundamental to what makes pottery so rewarding. Whether it's a functional bowl, a set of tableware, or a fun mug, each piece reflects the time, effort and creativity they poured into it.
