From Code to Clay:

How Pottery is Captivating San Francisco's Young Tech Workers

by Alyssa Frederick, Ashley Kim, and Taewon Yoon

[Enter Title]

As a newcomer to San Francisco, 26-year-old Tommy Li didn’t anticipate falling in love with pottery. A lot of his friends were doing it, but the high cost deterred him from signing up. “It’s not a cheap hobby,” he said. “But eventually I signed up, and once I started, I really enjoyed it.” He’s now been doing pottery for about eight months.

Like many young professionals living in the Bay Area, Li works in tech. As a product designer at Atlassian, he spends most of his day in front of a screen — be it at the office or at home. “Pottery feels like a third space for me,” he said.

Third spaces are places outside of home and work where people gather and establish community. They are particularly relevant for young tech workers in San Francisco, many of whom work remotely and don’t have established communities.

[POTTERY IS SOCIAL]

Meg Frazier, a program manager at Adobe in her late 20s, agreed with Li’s sentiment. “Pottery gets me out of the house,” she said. “After college, I wasn’t hanging out in communal spaces with friends as often as I used to, and after COVID, the motivation for me to go out was even less.”

Though Frazier works in-person twice a week, a majority of her work is done online. Hitting the bar with colleagues after work isn’t uncommon, but pottery is a nice space to have as someone who doesn’t enjoy drinking all the time, Frazier said. She also enjoys having a friend group separate from work.

Michela Ferrari, a mid-20s product designer at Comun, also started pottery after the pandemic. With classes running for six weeks at a time, Ferrari quickly formed friendships with regulars, including classmates and studio owners. Though most friendships have remained in-studio, she enjoys having pottery as her third space.

[POTTERY IS TACTILE]

Ferrari appreciates how working with her hands is a more physical and unstructured form of creativity compared to her digital design work. “There’s something very primitive about having clay in your hands,” she said. “It’s a nice sensation to have something physically bonding.”

Similarly, Li described pottery as a refreshing change from Zoom meetings all day. “It’s a hands-on, time-sensitive activity that requires focus and a different kind of problem-solving mindset,” he said. Li likened it to the extracurricular activities he used to do as a kid: a way to unplug and destress.

[POTTERY IS EXPENSIVE]

With one-time classes around $120 per person and class packages anywhere from $250 to $900, pottery isn’t cheap — even for young professionals making six figures in tech. Especially for those nervous to learn a new skill, pottery has high barriers to entry.

Jenna, a 23-year-old associate product manager at Hubspot, pays $100 per month for her gym membership — something she considers an investment in her mental and physical health. “Pottery is super cool, but I’d rather save up to travel and eat out,” she said. Jenna pointed out that working out, like pottery, can be social and personally rewarding.

Li participates in intramural volleyball in his free time, but still invests in pottery as something he specifically finds creatively stimulating. “I enjoy its relaxed pace,” he said. “But overall, I’d say having both creative and physical hobbies has helped me maintain a healthy work-life balance and increased my productivity at work.”

Despite pottery being expensive, Frazier likes how membership at her pottery studio gives her access to a reliably warm, feminine space. As a member of Stone Gold Pottery in Marina, she even has keys to lock up so she can leave when she wishes. “There’s a real sense of trust and mutual ownership here,” she said.

[POTTERY IS REWARDING]

Corgi Cup

Li, Frazier and Ferrari all shared an appreciation for the physicality of pottery. “It’s truly a process,” Frazier said. “Pottery takes time and commitment, but that’s what makes it so satisfying when you finish a project.”

Large Bowl

Both Li and Ferrari described how they’ve integrated pottery into their everyday lives. Li uses a large bowl of his own making to hold his Lactaid pills, which he takes on a daily basis. Ferrari also enjoys making functional tableware — including a cup with a corgi design inside that becomes visible when drinking.

Something Cool

Community is another crucial aspect of why pottery is so rewarding. Li, Frazier and Ferrari signed up for classes in their residential areas out of convenience. “I often run into people from the studio at local cafes or parks,” Li said. “It wasn’t intentional, but pottery has definitely increased my sense of community in the area.”

Something Else

Beyond finding community and producing beautiful designs, Ferrari comes back to the personal fulfillment of making with her hands. “It’s more challenging and requires more patience than drawing or rug making,” she said. “Pottery is a never-ending challenge, and you can always do more stuff and expand.”
